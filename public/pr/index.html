01 / Diagnostic

Fly91's Hubballi statement. Read line by line.

On April 20, Flight IC 3401 held for an hour over Hubballi, diverted to Bengaluru, and eventually returned. The plane was safe. The statement that followed was not. What Fly91 said about Hubballi is exactly how Fly91 should never speak again.

Every phrase below is drawn verbatim from CEO Manoj Chacko's public response and Fly91's official communication. Read together, they tell the 22 passengers on board: you are the problem.

Phrase 1
"The claims are incorrect and malicious."
The diagnosis: Opens by attacking the passengers who lived through it. "Malicious" is a word you use about an enemy — not 22 people who were crying in a cabin. This is the tonal centre of the problem: Fly91 treated the people inside the aircraft as opposition.
Phrase 2
"We urge responsible reporting."
The diagnosis: Scolds the media and, by extension, everyone sharing passenger videos. Teaches the audience that Fly91's first instinct in a crisis is to tell them to behave. This phrase alone will cost more goodwill than the incident itself.
Phrase 3
"Standard operating procedure. Diversions are routine."
The diagnosis: Technically true. Humanly deaf. "Routine" is the airline's word for an event that, for a first-time flyer, will be the most terrifying three hours of their life. This sentence confused routine for us with routine for them.
Phrase 4
"Impeccable safety record. Zero compromise on safety."
The diagnosis: The passengers did not say Fly91 was unsafe. They said Fly91 did not talk to them. Answering the question no one asked is a form of not listening. This phrase is also self-congratulatory during a moment that calls for humility.
Phrase 5
"Brief turbulence. Within normal pre-monsoon conditions."
The diagnosis: Dismisses what the passengers felt. A three-and-a-half-hour flight for a scheduled one-hour hop is not brief to anyone in the cabin. Language like this teaches the public that Fly91's emotional dial does not match theirs.
What's missing
Not a single "we're sorry." Not a single "we hear you." Not a single "that was frightening for you." Not a single "we should have communicated better in the cabin." Not a single "here is what we will change."
The diagnosis: The entire statement is addressed to regulators and press. It is not addressed to the 22 people it is about. That is the whole problem, in one observation.
02 / The rewrite

The statement Fly91 should have issued.

Same facts. Same facts. But ordered around the passenger, not around the procedure. This is the reply that would have ended the story on the day it began.

What Fly91 said · April 20

"The claims being circulated about Flight IC 3401 are incorrect and malicious. There was no technical snag on the aircraft."

"The diversion to Bengaluru was a standard operating procedure due to adverse weather. Our crew is highly trained and our safety record is impeccable. Zero compromise on safety."

"The turbulence was brief and within normal pre-monsoon conditions. We urge responsible reporting of facts."

What Fly91 should have said

"Yesterday, our flight from Hyderabad to Hubballi took three and a half hours instead of one. That was a frightening afternoon for the 22 people on board. We're sorry."

"The weather near Hubballi closed in before we could land. Our captain held above the airport for as long as it was safe, then made the call to divert to Bengaluru. That's the decision we wanted her to make. It's also the decision that kept you in the air far longer than we promised."

"Where we failed you was in the cabin. We didn't tell you enough, often enough, in language that sounded human. That is on us — not on the weather, not on the crew, not on the aircraft. And it's what we're changing first."

"From next week, every Fly91 flight that holds or diverts will have two things: a short update from the flight deck every ten minutes, in plain Hindi and English, and a call from our team to every passenger within an hour of landing. We will tell you what happened, before you have to ask."

"To everyone who flew IC 3401 on April 20 — we owe you an apology in person, not a press note. Our team will call each of you this week. We'd like to fly you again, on us, when you're ready."

"— Manoj Chacko, CEO, Fly91"

What the rewrite does that the original didn't

  • Opens with the passenger experience, not the company procedure. "Frightening" is a passenger word — use it.
  • Says "we're sorry" by the second sentence. Three times in total, unhedged.
  • Owns the actual failure: not the diversion, but the communication vacuum in the cabin. This is the diagnosis the passengers themselves gave.
  • Commits to specific change with a timeline. "From next week" beats "zero compromise" every day.
  • Ends with a personal gesture — individual calls, free rebooking — rather than an abstract claim about safety records.
  • Signs off as a named person. The CEO's name, not "the Fly91 team." Accountability has a face.
03 / Voice playbook

Five rules, every word we write. No exceptions.

This is the test every Fly91 tweet, statement, email, and boarding announcement passes before it leaves the building. If it fails even one rule, rewrite.

Rule 01
Acknowledge emotion first. Always.

Before procedure, before facts, before reassurance — name what the passenger is feeling. "A delay is the last thing you want to hear." "This must have been frightening." "You waited too long for an answer." The sentence after that can be a fact. The first sentence cannot.

Rule 02
Explain, don't justify.

Transparency beats reassurance. Tell passengers what's actually happening — the wind at the airport, the aircraft rotating in, the cabin crew briefing — in plain words. Defensive language ("as per procedure," "for operational reasons") is the audible sound of someone hiding.

Rule 03
Commit to a change, not a claim.

"We're sorry" is the opening line. "Here's what we're changing by Monday" is the closing line. Every crisis note ends with a specific, dated, verifiable action. Passengers don't want our feelings — they want proof we've learned.

Rule 04
Never speak to the media over the passenger's head.

Statements that address "the press" in the third person — *"we urge responsible reporting"* — teach the public that we care about coverage more than about people. Write as though one passenger is reading. If media pick it up, that's a bonus, not the brief.

Rule 05
Sign everything with a name.

No more statements from "Fly91" or "the airline." Every crisis note, apology, or change announcement carries a named human's byline — CEO, Head of Operations, Head of Customer Experience. Accountability has a face or it doesn't exist.

Bonus
Write to one passenger, not to everyone.

Every sentence should work if you imagine it being read by one nervous flyer in seat 12A. If it sounds like a press release to that person, rewrite it. Plural audiences are an illusion — people read singular.

04 / Incident response protocol

What to say, and when. Every time.

The next Hubballi will happen. Weather, tech, crew — something. The difference between a repeat of April 20 and a story that dies in 24 hours is whether this protocol kicks in.

The 90-minute protocol

From the moment anything goes wrong — delay > 30 minutes, diversion, incident — this sequence runs. No exceptions. No waiting for a legal review.

T + 0 min
In-cabin: "You deserve to know."

Flight deck or crew makes a plain-language announcement. No "due to operational reasons." Name the actual cause (weather / traffic / tech), name the decision being made, give a time window, acknowledge the inconvenience.

T + 10 min
Every 10 minutes, another update.

Even if nothing has changed. Silence is the enemy. "No new update — weather is still closed, we're still holding, we'll check again in ten" is infinitely better than twenty minutes of nothing.

T + 30 min
External post: short, human, signed.

Single social post (X, Instagram, WhatsApp status) from @fly91. Three sentences max. Names what happened, names what we're doing, names who to contact. Signed by a human, not "the team."

T + 60 min
Individual outreach starts.

Telentir places calls to every passenger on the affected flight — plus any connected travellers. Each call opens with "I'm sorry," explains what happened, offers options. The call happens before the passenger has to find us.

T + 90 min
Full statement from the CEO.

Long-form post — website, X thread, email to booked passengers. Includes: what went wrong, why it did, what we're changing, who's responsible. Signed by the CEO, not "the airline." The passage Fly91 did not publish for Hubballi.

05 / The campaign

"Flying, Explained." A content commitment, not a campaign.

The best long-term answer to a viral panic video is a library of videos that teach people what flying actually feels like — before they board. Fly91 should be the airline that publishes the content IndiGo won't.

The campaign is simple: for the next twelve months, every fear, misconception, or invisible aviation decision becomes a 45–90 second piece of content. Real pilots. Real passengers. Real explanations. No music swell. No corporate gloss. Just "here's what's happening, here's why, here's what you'll feel."

Launch idea: "Why Your Plane Circled"

One short film, released the week the pitch is approved. Narrated, calm, 55 seconds. Explains what a hold is, why pilots choose them, what passengers feel. Posted without reference to Hubballi — but every viewer will make the connection. This is how Fly91 responds to the incident without ever mentioning it again.

From there, one new "Flying, Explained" piece per week for twelve months. 52 short films that together become the most-watched aviation content in India — the Fly91 YouTube channel becomes the thing first-time flyers send each other before they fly.

Why it works for Fly91 specifically

  • It matches the network. Fly91 serves tier-2/3 cities — where a disproportionate share of first-time flyers take off. Content meets audience where they are.
  • It's a moat bigger airlines won't build. IndiGo, Air India, Akasa won't make "Why the plane is loud" content. It's too close to admitting weakness. Fly91 can own it because Fly91 isn't pretending.
  • It compounds. Each video is evergreen. A passenger searching "why does a plane circle" a year from now lands on Fly91's explanation. That's demand capture, disguised as education.
06 / Four content pillars

What we publish, and why.

Four columns holding up the "Flying, Explained" campaign. Each one answers a question Fly91's audience is already asking — and none of the bigger airlines is answering.

Pillar 01 Educational
How flying actually works.

The explainer pillar. Turbulence. Go-arounds. Holding patterns. Why ATRs sound different. What "light chop" actually means. What the cabin crew do in those first two minutes after takeoff. Short, calm, accurate, non-condescending.

Formats: 45–90s videos · long-form Instagram carousels · pilot-voice audio snippets · in-app pop-ups during booking
Pillar 02 Route as story
The cities we fly to, told properly.

Every Fly91 destination gets a series. Sindhudurg isn't a beach on a price chart — it's a Konkan coast that used to take a day. Agatti isn't an airport code — it's the only flight to Lakshadweep. The route pages are stories, not fares. This is the demand-capture layer of the network, done with soul.

Formats: editorial route pages (SEO) · 3-minute city films · weekend-trip itineraries · local-led travel guides
Pillar 03 People
The humans on the aircraft.

The captain who flew her first route from Goa to Agatti. The cabin-crew lead who speaks four South Indian languages. The ground-staff team at Jalgaon who know every weekly flyer by name. Trust is a face, not a fleet. Fly91's people are its competitive advantage — make them visible.

Formats: portrait profiles · "day in the life" mini-docs · Instagram takeovers · in-app crew cards on booking
Pillar 04 Transparency
"We were wrong, and here's what changed."

A quarterly post from the CEO. Names specific things Fly91 got wrong that quarter — a mishandled delay, a complaint pattern, a policy that had to change. Describes what's different now. No other Indian airline does this. It is an enormous trust-generator, and nearly free.

Formats: CEO-signed essay posts · internal town-hall excerpts · year-in-review long read
07 / Social response templates

Replies that match the rules. Copy-paste-ready.

Every social reply follows the same three-step architecture: acknowledge → explain → act. Below are the five scenarios Fly91 faces weekly, with a sample incoming post and the response that gets sent within 20 minutes.

Scenario A · Delay complaint
"@fly91 my flight to Pune is delayed 3 hours and NOBODY at the gate is telling us anything. This is the third time."
Response (within 20 min): "Ameya — three hours is a long time, and silence makes it worse. I'm really sorry. I'm checking the flight now and will come back to you in 5 minutes with the cause, the new time, and what we can do for you. Your booking ref? — Priya, Fly91 CX team"
Scenario B · Safety concern / viral panic
"FLY91 flight was circling Hubballi for an HOUR. People crying. This airline is a JOKE. Never again. 🚨"
Response: "You're right to be shaken. An hour in a hold, with no one explaining what was happening, is frightening — and unacceptable. Our captain was waiting for weather to clear before landing, which was the right call. Telling you that in real time was our job, and we didn't do it. I'm so sorry. Can I DM you? I want to share what we're changing, and I'd like to get you home on us when you're ready to fly again. — Manoj, CEO"
Scenario C · Cancellation frustration
"@fly91 cancelled my flight 2 hours before departure. I have a wedding tomorrow. What the hell."
Response: "A wedding, and two hours' notice — that's the worst possible combination. I'm genuinely sorry. DM us your PNR right now. I'll personally route you via our next open seat on any carrier that gets you there tomorrow, at our cost. No forms. No waiting. — Ritu, Fly91"
Scenario D · First-flight nervousness
"flying fly91 tomorrow for the first time ever and i'm so anxious someone please tell me it will be okay 😭"
Response: "Sent a DM. Short version here: your aircraft is called an ATR 72, propellers you can see, smaller cabin. Takeoff feels quick, the engines sound mechanical (normal), and you'll feel air more than in a big jet. All of it is okay. If you want, we'll call you tonight and walk through the whole thing. You'll land in Sindhudurg less nervous than when you boarded. Promise. — Sana, Fly91"
Scenario E · Positive review / unprompted praise
"Just flew fly91 Goa to Hyderabad. Crew was kind, pilot explained the turbulence before it hit, we landed early. Not an ad. Just rare."
Response: "Shalini, thank you. This is the bar we want to hit every flight — not the exceptional one. Sending this to the crew that flew you. If you're open to it, we'd love to share your words on our routes page (with your name or anonymous — your call). — Fly91"

Golden rule on all of these: Reply in under 20 minutes, from a named human, with one concrete thing you're doing. If you can't solve it on-thread, move it to DM in the first reply.

08 / Customer recovery

The unhappy customer is the most persuadable person in India.

Most airlines treat a vocal complaint as a problem to contain. Fly91 should treat it as the cheapest lead-generation Fly91 will ever run. An ex-detractor saying "they actually called, listened, fixed it, and I flew again" is worth more than any campaign.

The goal is not silencing. The goal is conversion. Every passenger who posted a bad experience in the last 90 days gets identified, contacted, and given a real chance to become an advocate. Done properly, recovery produces the most credible marketing Fly91 can buy — because it's not marketing.

Step 01
Identify

Social listening across X, Instagram, Google Reviews, MakeMyTrip, Ixigo. Name every passenger with a public complaint in last 90 days. Aim: 50 names in week one.

Step 02
Reach

Direct outreach from a named human — not a support ticket. Call or DM. Opens with "I saw what you wrote. I'm sorry it happened." Not "Thank you for your feedback."

Step 03
Repair

Offer something real, matched to the complaint: free future flight, category upgrade, written apology from the CEO, named callback, crew-level feedback loop. Real, not performative.

Step 04
Convert

Invite to become a story. Permission to share their journey from angry to loyal. Publish as a short case study — in their voice, not ours.

Example: Ameya Kunte — the Goa→Pune delay passenger

Ameya is a named complainant from April 2025 (Fly91 IC 1378 delay, 4 hours, no refreshments, tarmac walk at Pune). His specific complaints: no communication, no refreshments, unsafe disembarkation. A recovery move looks like this:

  • A personal call from a Fly91 ops manager — not customer support. Acknowledges the specific issues. Asks what the best thing we could do now would be.
  • A round-trip flight on any Fly91 route he chooses, for him and someone he loves. No forms. No expiry.
  • A promise, signed: on his next Fly91 flight, a crew member will greet him by name at boarding. He'll see his feedback reflected in the cabin.
  • A "first, do no harm" follow-up: three months after he flies with us again, we email to ask if it was better — and we publish his answer verbatim, whatever it is.

The cost of this is a few thousand rupees in fare. The value is a publicly published sequence where a named, specific, vocal critic describes how Fly91 won them back. That's not PR. That's evidence.

09 / The ban list

Words Fly91 doesn't use anymore.

The vocabulary that got Fly91 into the Hubballi hole. From this week on, any communication with these words gets rewritten before it goes out.

Delete these.

Each of these is a word Fly91 used in the Hubballi response — or is a phrase the rest of Indian aviation leans on. They sound professional. They land as cold.

"Malicious" Attacks the passenger. Never describes a complaint this way.
"Incorrect" If they experienced it, it's real to them. Use "different from what we saw."
"Responsible reporting" Scolds the audience. Never tell the public how to report.
"Impeccable" Self-congratulation during a crisis. Arrogant by default.
"Zero compromise" Corporate-speak. Replace with specifics of what was done.
"Standard operating procedures" Procedural deflection. Name the actual procedure, in English.
"As per our policy" Hides behind paper. Explain the reason the policy exists.
"Regrettably" A word spoken by companies, not people. Use "I'm sorry."
"Routine" Routine for us. Never routine for the passenger living it.
"Inconvenience" Too small for most of what we're apologising for. Use the real word.
"Operational reasons" A black box. Name the actual reason: weather, crew, traffic, aircraft.
"The team" Faceless. Every statement is signed by a specific human.
